## Mostly Hydrocarbons
Crude Oil is a mixture of different hydrocarbons + other minor ingredients.
Hydrocarbon = Hydrogen + Carbon.
But in Crude Oil, these hydrocarbons vary in size & structure. From small (like methane (CH4) which has 1 carbon atom & 4 hydrogen atoms -- to giant ones with 80+ carbon atoms (more closer to tar).

---
## Elemental Analysis of Crude Oil
Carbon -> 11%-15% by mass
Hydrogen -> 83% to 87% by mass
+other stuff like sulphur, nitrogen,..

Hydrogen to Carbon (H:C) ratio is around 1.8
H:C ratio of Bituminous Coal -> 0.8
H:C ratio of Methane -> 4

## What Are These Various Types of Hydrocarbons?
Three types dominate (90%+ by weight) the composition (of crude oil).
Scientific names of these series are **alkanes**, **cycloalkanes**, and arenes.
In the Crude Oil industry they are known as **paraffins**, **cycloparaffins (or naphthenes)** and **aromatics**.

---

**Cycloalkanes** 
- most abundant compounds in crude oil.
- with methylcyclopentane and methylcyclohexane present in the greatest quantities.
- ring-shaped & more complex than alkanes.
- carbon atoms joined in rings of five (cyclopentane) or six (cyclohexane) atoms.
- Found mostly in heavier oils and are important in making jet fuel or diesel.

**Alkanes** 
- second most abundant compounds.
- inert nature (are stable): they do not react with either strong acids or alkaline (or oxygenating) compounds.
- Chains with 1 to 4 carbons are gas. Like Methane (cooking gas), Ethane (industrial feedstock for making plastics), Propane (BBQ grills and portable heaters sold in cylinders) & Butane (lighters & small camping stoves)
- Chains with 5 to 16 carbons are liquid (like pentane, hexane – similar to paint thinners or solvents– to hexadecane).
- Chains with more than 16 carbons become waxy solids (like Vaseline or candle wax).
- 2 types of alkanes – **Straight-chain** and **Branched-chain**.
- Straight-chain: Methane, Ethane, Propane, Butane
- Branched-chain: generally isomers of straight-chain alkanes i.e. same molecular formula but different structure due to the branching of the carbon chain

![[alkane-branched.png]]
**Arenes**
- unsaturated, highly reactive liquids.
- hydrocarbons with at least one benzene ring – a special hexagonal ring made of six carbon atoms with alternating double bonds
- benzene is present together with its alkyl derivatives (toluene, ethylbenzene and xylene).
- Aromatic doesn't mean they smell nice (though some do), it refers to their structure.
- Polycyclic aromatics — naphthalenes, anthracene and phenanthrene — are most common in heavy oils and lubricants and they make up less than 20% of crude oil mass.

## Sulfur: The Most Common Contaminant
Sulfur (ultimately derived from sulfur bonds present in proteins of ancient organic matter) is the most common undesirable contaminant of crude oils because its combustion generates sulfur dioxide, a leading precursor of acid rain. So-called ‘sour’ oils have more than 2% of sulfur (which means that in residual oils and in asphalt the levels are commonly above 5%), while ‘sweet’ crude oils have less than 0.5%, with some of them (from Nigeria, Australia and Indonesia) having less than 0.05% S.
